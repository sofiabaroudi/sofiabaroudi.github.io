Sofia Baroudi

When Time Split

August 5, 2025

I write because I find no solace in silence.I write because the shattered glass tells a story my voice cannot carry. August 4th 2020 marked itself in microseconds—first the stillness, then the pressure wave that turned every window in Beirut into deadly rain. I was in my car when the sky cracked open, when the ordinary afternoon transformed into a moment that would divide time into before and after.

The sound came first, not as sound but as silence—a vacuum that pulled at my eardrums before the blast pushed everything sideways. The windshield held, barely, but spiderwebbed into a thousand crystalline paths. All around, the city’s glass skin fell like sharp snow.

I remember thinking: this is how a city weeps.

We used to hear warnings about the port’s mismanagement from my grandfather’s voice, his words carrying across Sunday lunch tables. We used to think storing ammonium nitrate near a civilian population was just another Lebanese tale of bureaucratic negligence. I now realize that negligence is not only a euphemism but a deliberate offense to our livelihoods.

To quantify such a moment would be pointless. To explain how a single instant can rewrite memory, how the smell of burning port air can etch itself into your dreams, how the sounds of car alarms and sirens merged into one long keening—these things resist ordinary language. We measured disaster in the missing: missing glass, missing walls, missing people who should have been answering their phones.

I cannot carry on without acknowledging that my survival was geography, was chance, was the difference between being in a car rather than beside a window, between driving on this street rather than that one. I cannot carry on living a privileged life in the sanctity of certainty when families who lost everything still wait for answers. I cannot think of my afternoon drive without remembering how ordinary moments become extraordinary horror. My neighbour was my age when she first opened her beauty salon near the port; now its windows are just frames holding absence.

I understand that time is not linear but circular—we carry these moments with us, sharp-edged and refracting light.

Today, the city still bears its scars like memory.

We have learned to read history in the boarding of windows, in the empty frames that once held glass. But we have also learned how hope moves faster than fear—how neighbors become family in the space of a heartbeat, how hands that help need no common language, how resilience flows in the bloodstream of this ancient shore.
